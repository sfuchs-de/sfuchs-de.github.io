How freight
finds another way

What happens after a road improves, a terminal clogs, or an entire mode disappears?

Freight does not simply stop. It changes routes, switches modes, loads new terminals, and alters congestion elsewhere. This walkthrough shows why those adjustments determine the economic and environmental value of transport infrastructure.

01

One shipment,
several networks

A container traveling inland may begin on a truck, transfer to rail, and return to a truck near its destination. Each transfer joins two networks at a terminal.

The relevant choice is therefore not only which route to take. It is which route and which mode to use at every step.

Press the alternatives below to see three ways the same shipment can cross the country.

More on recursive routing

At every node, the model summarizes all onward sourcing, route, and mode options in a continuation value. This turns a high-dimensional path problem into a recursive sequence of local choices while preserving the full network.

02

A cheaper road changes every mode

When road transport becomes cheaper, freight shifts from rail toward trucks on the affected corridor. But the cheaper composite link also attracts additional traffic from elsewhere in the network.

The first response is modal substitution. The second is network complementarity.

Illustrative modal shares use the paper's estimated substitution elasticity of 1.099. A positive road-cost change raises the rail-to-road traffic ratio.

How the elasticity is estimated

The paper combines confidential U.S. rail waybill data with truck traffic and uses changes in interstate-highway capacity as a road-cost shifter. A 1% increase in interstate highways reduces relative rail-to-truck traffic by roughly 0.9–1.2%.

03

Switching modes takes space and time

Ports and inland terminals are where freight changes networks. When more traffic reaches a terminal, ships, trains, cranes, and storage space compete for limited capacity.

Using minute-level vessel-positioning data, the paper finds that a 1% increase in port traffic raises ship dwell time by approximately 0.24–0.26%.

From dwell times to congestion costs

The empirical dwell-time response maps into a terminal-congestion elasticity of 0.096 in the calibrated transport-cost system. The same congestion mechanism is applied at coastal ports and inland rail terminals.

04

A local change travels through the network

Improving one link or terminal creates a direct response nearby. The resulting change in composite transport costs then redirects traffic, shifts congestion, and changes market access farther away.

Central terminals matter because many routes depend on them and close substitutes are scarce.

Choose a location to see the footprint of a 1% reduction in its switching cost.

Substitution and complementarity

Direct substitution shifts traffic toward a cheaper mode and away from competing modes on the same link. Indirect complementarity arises because the link's lower composite cost makes every route and sourcing relationship that uses it more attractive.

What the network adds

A local infrastructure change matters through the alternatives it creates—and through the congestion those alternatives produce.

Flexibility creates value. Additional modes and routes let freight bypass disrupted or expensive links.

Flexibility is not free. Reallocated traffic can load other roads and terminals, dissipating part of the gain.

Location matters. Central terminals and links with few close alternatives have the largest network footprints.

Reference

Mode
A transport technology: road, rail, or water.
Route
A sequence of connected links used to move freight between origin and destination.
Intermodal terminal
A port or inland facility where freight transfers between modes.
Modal substitution
Traffic shifting toward a mode whose relative generalized cost has fallen.
Network complementarity
Additional traffic attracted because a cheaper link improves every route and sourcing relationship that uses it.
Switching cost
The generalized cost of entering or leaving a non-road mode at a terminal.
Market access
A summary of how easily a location can reach suppliers and consumers through the equilibrium network.
